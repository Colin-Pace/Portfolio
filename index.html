Profile

I program both single page, interactive, asynchronous web applications that have responsive design for phones, tablets, and computers, and also in general purpose programming languages. The technology that I use for web development is HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and SQL often with the frameworks of React and Express for the server and client and PostgreSQL for the database. Otherwise I use Java mostly, but also C++.

My first project, Web Text, provides the opportunity to store notes in folders once the account of a user is registered, authenticated, and authorized. It is hosted on Heroku. I wrote my second project, Minesweeper, in plain JavaScript. It is hosted on GitHub pages. Another project is a basic full stack implementation. It is written in plain JavaScript on the client side. The source code is on GitHub in two repositories, one for the client and another for the server and database, which were written with Express and PostgreSQL.

In addition to these projects, I have written in Java or JavaScript, translated from Java into JavaScript, or in a few instances adapted from JavaScript more than 150 algorithms. The data structures of the algorithms range from arrays and linked lists to stacks and graphs. I found prompts for them in different places, like Cracking the Coding Interview, LeetCode, HackerRank, freeCodeCamp, and InterviewBit. The algorithms are collected in a repository on my GitHub account. In another repository, there are my implementations of introductory programs of neural networks. I found the programs in various tutorials on YouTube. I wrote them in Python. Other projects on GitHub include an adaptation of a web crawler in JavaScript, a series of recreations of the front end of websites, and a series of grid games that are templates built from a template given by a freeCodeCamp video. A description of each and its link can be found on my resume. Of particular note among them is a grid game that uses Dijkstra's algorithm to find the shortest path in a grid of elements between an opponent and player as they move on a wraparound board. In August 2020, I wrote a book titled Algorithms and Programs. It is a rough draft under continual revision.

I began to learn programming in 2018. At the time, I was a graduate student at The University of Texas at Austin in The Department of Anthropology. I had studied many languages at UT. Of the languages I studied at the university, I took the most courses in Hindi. I studied it also for four semesters at The American Institute of Indian Studies in Jaipur, Rajasthan. The Director of The Hindi Urdu Flagship at UT supervised my master's degree report. He also is a co-author of the popular textbook Teach Yourself Hindi. The book is unique in its clarity of exposition. From him, I learned Hindi and the technique of writing instructional material. I tried to incorporate the style into my book, Algorithms and Programs. In The Deparment of Anthropology, my mentor was a graduate of The Committe on Social Thought at The University of Chicago. During his education, he worked with luminaries in the field such as Victor Turner, Benedict Anderson, Claude Levi-Strauss, and Clifford Geertz. Even though he was restrained about relating anecdotes, the ideas filtered through an interpretation of them in his work, and I experimented with them in mine. It might be worth noting that Turner influenced anthropology and literature more generally through the concepts of communitas and liminality. Liminality was an idea that inspired Chuck Palahniuk while he wrote Fight Club. I had read the book as a student in high school. I once even attended his reading of his short story, "Guts," at the Barnes and Noble at the Arboretum in Austin, TX. Anyway, with good instruction, I found myself at a crossroads in The Department of Anthropology with a methodological decision.

The decision was whether I should pursue the discipline of anthropology or another that is more focused on language. Because of my deep and abiding passion for morphology and syntax, in 2018, as an exploration into a potential new direction, I began to learn to program. I started with Python and immediately enjoyed it immensely. After working through a few textbooks, I changed languages, learning the syntax of JavaScript, HTML, CSS, and SQL. I started to program in Java and C++. With these experiences, I was convinced that I wanted to become a programmer.

I write on Twitter about the scientific topics of climate change, evolution, and expansion.
