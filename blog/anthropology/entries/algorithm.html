al-Khwarizmi's name was translated as Algorismus

From the nineteenth century, computer scientists have used the term algorithm to describe an order of operations that if enacted upon data contained in a structure thereby achieves a desired result. An example is a program called hello world. The phrase hello world is placed inside quotation marks, which are called a string, and is given to a function. The function is ordered to print the contents of the string to the monitor. The algorithm is the steps of creating the string, passing it to the function, and printing the string.

Algorithm has an interesting etymology. It was borrowed into English from 15th century Latin algorithmus. Algorithmus or its variant spelling algorismus derive from a proper name, al-Khwarizmi. Khwarizmi was a Persian mathematician who lived in the ninth century AD. He worked at the House of Wisdom. There he wrote The compendious book on calculation by completion and balancing, more or less inventing algebra. In the thirteenth century, as European intellectuals started to translate Arabic texts into Latin, they took his name for that branch of mathematics.

Another book that Khwarizmi wrote was Concerning the Hindu art of reckoning. This book introduced Europe to Hindu-Arabic numerals. Hindu inscriptions from the early first millennium AD are the first attestations of this number system. A few centuries later, they were introduced to Arab scholars. Then, through Khwarizmi, the system went to Europe. EB adds that Khwarizmi's name was translated as Algorismus, whence the Latinate etymology.

What Khwarizmi's book on Hindu numbers doesn't elucidate, is the algorithm, as it is thought of today. So unlike the eponymous algebra, that of algorithm is inaccurate. Adding more confusion to the context, There was a translational error with algorismus. Apparently many mathematicians thought it was a derivation not from Khwarizmi but instead from the classical Greek term arithmos, which is the true etymon of English's arithmetic.

Leonardo Fibonacci, the thirteenth century Italian mathematician, was the first European during the Renaissance to write about Arabic mathematics, in his work Book of the abacus. The encyclopedia also credits him as the popularizer in Europe of the Hindu-Arabic numeral system that Khwarizmi introduced, when everyone was still counting with Roman numerals or Greek letters. By another curious historical chance, one of the popular algorithms today is called the Fibonacci sequence, which he discovered. Starting with 0 and 1, it adds each two numbers before a third, then building on itself. For example 0 and 1 are 1, 1 and 1 are 2, 2 and 1 are 3, and so on. Another accurate eponym.

Etymology is interesting because it shows lineages of lexical form. One can trace the series of letters from this language to that language, often through several languages, to some place where it was invented. Often the cognates share a common denotation. For example, the classical Greek systema, which combines the prefix syn- "with," sta- "to stand," and the nominal ending -ma, with the denotation of “composed of several parts,” became systema in Latin, sistema in Spanish, sistema in Russian, and system in English, among other languages, all with close to the same meaning.

Other times, a lexical form took on modifications and was imbued with different denotations during its historical vicissitudes. A good example is the classical Latin, Greek, and Sanskrit versions of the Proto-Indo-European *weyd- “to know, see.” The asterisk indicates that it is a reconstructed term, based on a common development: No text or inscription can attest its existence. In Latin, *weyd- developed into video “I see.” In Greek, it became idea, “notion, thought.” And Sanskrit created the term veda “knowledge.” The lexical forms and denotation are similar, though not the same. The *weyd- example thereby corresponds with that of algorithm, showing the degree of variability within etymology.
