The argument about global warming is a sensible opinion informed by science, but not science.

The early republics focused on balancing rationality, wealth, and militarism. The philosopher king idealized by Plato was educated in the earliest form of the trivium and quadrivium, as were the citizens. With enough life experience, by the age of fifty or so, he used knowledge to guide the city state to the same balance of mind, money, and muscle that he himself had cultivated.

By the time of Montesquieu and Madison some two millennia later, the republics changed the divisions but maintained the idea of balance. Philosophers insisted for checks on power to achieve a balance among the legislative, executive, and judicial branches of a government. In doing so, it was argued, tyranny was replaced by democracy.

Along with the reform of the republic, education of the population had changed from memorization of poetic devices in the stanzas of The Iliad and The Odyssey and the declension patterns of classical Latin, to discussions of the discovery of structure in nature and its potential applications for rational technology and government. The change was incomplete, however. Even Voltaire championed the Newtonian revolution among the elite at The Sun King's Versailles, couched in the language of messianism.

Another two hundred years on, and today some 40 percent of the American citizenry report their belief in young Earth creationism with humanity in its present form about 10,000 years ago, despite the demonstrable inaccuracy of the ideology.

Scientific literature is often dense and turgid, and for those reasons inaccessible and unaccessed. That is in addition to the abstract and therefore abstruse nature of the material.

Scientists and climate activists also tend to frame global warming in terms of cataclysm. Extreme weather and rising oceans will devastate civilization. It is the type of story that the religious country responds to. The story displays an indulgence among scientists for the messianic genre. But the doom and gloom narrative distracts from the scientific stories, the this causes that causes this and that sequences, that give science its explanatory power.

The scientific community's wavering stance on Karl Popper's instruction for falsifiability in experimentation, also discredits the enterprise. While the measurement of CO2 in the atmosphere, the statistical occurrence of extreme weather events, and the correspondence among them and the rise in global temperatures and oceanic levels, involve science, at no point is the theory of global warming availale for experimentation. So the argument is a sensible opinion informed by science, but not science.
