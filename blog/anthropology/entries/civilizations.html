23 April 2018

A persuasive argument for the existence of extraterrestrials, and moreover their presence here on Earth, is the independent and concurrent advent of civilization across the globe. Approximately 4,000 years ago in river valleys and along coasts, these civilizations developed: the Xia near the Yangtze, the Harappan in the Indus valley, the Sumerians amid the Tigris and Euphrates, the First Dynasty of Narmer in the Nilotic valley, the Greeks on the lowland coast between the Pindus range and the Aegean Sea, the Olmecs around the Tuxtla range below the Gulf of Mexico, and the Chavin between the Andes and the South Pacific Ocean.

While it is clear that humanity has a lineage from its emergence in the valley between the Eastern Rift range and the Indian ocean, to Asia a hundred thousand years ago, to Europe forty thousand years ago, and to the Americas twenty thousand years ago, what remains unclear is the impetus of the formation of civilizations within a thousand years of each other. It is not the recession of the last ice age that marked the end of the Pleistocene epoch. Humans migrated in the colder years. And there were ten thousand years of a warmer climate before the civilizations arose. What are the chances that humans by chance coevally gained the necessary conditions for civilizational evolution, then in each place to write stories about supernatural beings that administer our species?
