Given two premises, does the conclusion follow from them?

Logic has multiple denotations. There is a definition often modified by the adjective formal to denote a branch of science that concerns deduction. Another definition suggests a less rigorous connection among facts or events, as in when Spock says, “that is logical, captain.” A third definition is technical and concerns circuitry and a nineteenth century mathematician named George Boole.

Francis Bacon was the popularizer of modern science insofar as his book The new instrument, redirected logic from its Aristotelian form, which the latter posited in The instrument. What was new about Bacon's logic was a focus on induction or experiment to create an axiom, and the subsequent testing of axioms as they were interrelated. Aristotle and the scholastics for the 18 centuries before Bacon, were keen to discern only validity in their statements. Given two premises, does the conclusion follow from them? But the soundness of their logic was often dubious, for often a base of a premise was untrue.

The falsity of many premises derived from an unscientific acceptance of facts such as the idea that there were only four elements: earth, air, wind, and fire. One can understand why Aristotle thought these were basic, or even maybe relatable to the human personality. It is such dubiety, however, that Bacon rails against in The new instrument. So even though Aristotle in his natural philosophy introduced induction to Platonism, which posits forms of existence without proof, so too Bacon introduced a more rigorous induction to the tenuous theories of the Aristotelians.

That Bacon's method was adopted and elaborated during the next four centuries is attested today in modern scientific textbooks, which insist on the method of hypothesis, experiment, and conclusion with the addition of reproduction of the experiment by others in different places. What Bacon's new instrument did not do, is reinvent formal logic. Today scientists do not frame their hypotheses in the form of a syllogism. For example, in the abstract of their paper that announced the discovery of exoplanets, Wolszczan and Frail (1998) write that, “Using the 305-m Arecibo radiotelescope to make precise timing measurements of pulses from the recently discovered 6.2-ms pulsar PSR1257 +12, we demonstrate that, rather than being associated with a stellar object, the pulsar is orbited by two or more planet-sized bodies.” I find this style of prose clearer and more accessible than that of formal logic, and it carries the power of verifiability, introduced to the scientific method by Bacon's induction.

The term logic is built into at least two of the professional titles of the natural sciences, biology and geology or logicians of life and the planet. In popular culture, it is used mostly by Spock or someone else acting like Spock, saying that something is logical or illogical. If the popular usage follows “if … then …” reasoning, such as, “if it is cold, then I won't go outside,” which is a form of Boolean algebra, also called Boolean logic, then with induction, logic displays its new instrumentality.
