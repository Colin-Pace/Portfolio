The repair of mutated mitotic codons

Cancer is a disease that causes a cell to divide uncontrollably. This is abnormal. The subsequent divisions of the cell also so divide. Gradually, the cells develop into a tumor. Also, they can spread by the lymphatic or circulatory system into other areas of the body. Tumors that impinge on tissue and organs, potentially inhibit their function. If a tumor develops and then stops, then the tumor is called benign. A benign tumor is not cancer, but it had been while it divided erratically until a suppressor ended its divisions. A malignant tumor is one that divides and invades other cells. The presence of such malignancy is cancer.

Normal cellular division is carried out in one of two processes, meiosis or mitosis. Meiosis is for reproduction, and mitosis is for tissue growth. Both are governed by genetic information that is stored in a cell. In general, the structure of a cell is a membrane that contains cytoplasm and a cytoskeleton that hold and structure the cellular organelles. One of the organelles, the nucleus, contains chromosomes. A chromosome is an x-shaped structure that holds DNA. On a chromosome, DNA is compactly structured into nucleosomes. Nucleosomes comprise histones. Unraveled histones display nucleotides. A collection of three nucleotides, with the base pairs of guanine, cytosine, adenine, and thymine, is called a codon. A codon is the basic unit of DNA code.

Located in the deep, dense chromosomes, the genetic code of a human has approximately 25,000 genes. The responsibilities of the genes include the direction of a cell along its cycle, from duplication to apoptosis. During the cycle, DNA directs the recruitment of local factors in order to synthesize proteins to create organelles. From the nucleus, the genome also instructs proteins on inter- and intra- cellular communication and coordination along cascading signal transduction pathways.

The orders of magnitude in a cell are worth noting. A cell averages between dozens and a hundred micrometers in diameter. The DNA double helix is measured in naometers. An atomic nucleus has a radius of femtometers. An electron is about an attometer wide. A helpful analogy is that the ratio of size between an atom and an apple is about that between an apple and the Earth. So when talking about the microscopic environments of a cell, it should be kept in mind that a nucleotide is almost imperceptibly small in comparison to a cellular membrane.

During normal replication, there are often mistakes in replication and also mutations from mutagens. Of the 37 trillion cells in a body, each lasts between ten and five hundred days. Cells of eukaryotic organisms replicate at approximately 3,000 base pairs per minute. Every one and a few hundred million replications has an error. And there are 3 billion base pairs that encompass 46 chromosomes in the nucleus. During replication, DNA has protein assemblages called polymerases that identify and repair the erred transcription by excision or non-homologous joining. Tumor suppressor genes also repair mutations, which are the most common cause of cancer.

Because mitotic genes control mitosis, and because cancer is uncontrolled division, therefore a mutation of a mitotic codon gives rise to cancer. If an anti-oncogene fails to suppress hyperplasia and dysplasia, then the result is a malignant tumor. The most common response by oncology to stop cancerous growth, is the destruction of the malignancy by techniques like excision, radiation, and chemotherapy. Treatment of this sort can be found at major clinics like MD Anderson, the National Cancer Institute of National Institute of Health, and the American Cancer Society. Somewhat successful, nonetheless this approach is blunt and has difficult side effects for patients. Also, much of the literature focuses on accurate diagnosis of the type of tumor so that an appropriate course of medicine can be pursued.

A more promising technique, one that is precise and perhaps without the debilitating side effects, is emerging in the field of biochemistry. Genetic sequencing and editing technology, has been developed for general use, and can now be applied oncologically. Sequencers are can map the genetic code of a person. Once identified, a tumor can be identified by the organ it grew in. This process becomes complex. With each replication, a cancerous cell might distort to an increasing degree the mutation, causing even stranger genetic configurations in its successors.

Because there are only 200 cellular types in the body, and because they tend to group around organs, therefore the deduction of the type of cancerous tissue can be made. Once the type is determined, then genetic editors can join them in the tumor microenvironment. There the editors can perform the standard operations of base excision and non-homologous end joining so that the chromosomes of cancerous cells become normal again in their genetic code. Current technology available for oncology includes sequencers like Illumina by NanoString Technology and CRISPR-CAS 9 by the Doudna Lab.
