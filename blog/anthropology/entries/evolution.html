Evolution might be modeled as levels of interrelated competitions.

The other day, I searched YouTube for a video that shows microevolution. Flies have demonstrated morphological change, and perhaps even speciation. Surely someone has a time lapse video.

The idea that competition, reproduction, mutation, and speciation take place is often told about a species in a habitat. The finches developed beaks that allow them to access food stored in trees.

The connection of competition is present for all species in an ecosystem. If each individual plant and animal participates in the trophic cycle, they compete with their own species for whatever food source constitutes their diet, and with other species that subsist on it. That continual competition in turn alters those species that prey on them. And so on.

Evolution might be modeled as levels of interrelated competitions. The first competition is among those species that share a food source. The interrelated competitions include how any one competition affects others. The rate of evolutionary change in one species could be measured in relation to those of others, and then to the ecosystem as an entirety. I wonder if biologists talk about this. The closest discourse I've found is parallel evolution.
