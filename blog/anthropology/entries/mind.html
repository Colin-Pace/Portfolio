The pitch of a person's voice goes up and down without reason

Dictionaries write that the mind is a subjective collection of memory, knowledge, belief, idea, and emotion. The definition can be elaborated. Subjectivity is the locality of experience in an individual body and its sensory perception. Memory is the recall of previous knowledge. Knowledge is information gained from experience. Belief is trust in the truthfulness of something. An idea is a concept of a thing. And emotion is a dispositional response to an event or object.

Meditation controls the mental collection by focusing its attention to a single event or object. In doing so, a person gains awareness of fluctuation. Not only is there sensory perception that continually draws attention away from contemplation, but there is also emotion and pattern of thought that stimulates emotion, giving a person temporary pleasure.

While it is possible to function without sustained awareness of mental direction and self reflection, nonetheless there are attributes that rarely otherwise come to the foreground. For example, during speech, the pitch of a person's voice goes up and down without reason. Sometimes people suggest that varying pitch avoids monotony, which is true, but at the same time it obscures intentionality, because it is unclear why a word or phrase is so emphasized. More often than not, it seems the person repeats pitch patterns that characterize their speaking style. The same might be said for vocal timbre, head movement, and facial expression. Often people shake their head side to side or up and down, or grin or scowl while speaking or listening to others. It's not boring, sure, but also it adds complexity and usually without the speaker's intention.

One might apprehend pitch and muscular comportment as its own expression. In addition to listening to the content of a message, we can listen to tone, and understand that even if it doesn't directly link to the morpheme that it temporally corresponds with, nonetheless the tone sets the atmosphere in which the content takes place. The person seems happy, and talks about their day, is an example of such an interpretation.

The more I think about how often I thoughtlessly speak and move, the more I appreciate the formal and reserved style that before I perceived as stilted and insipid. Perhaps it is stilted and insipid, but this style narrows the channels of expressive denotation, focusing on the lexemes. I see clarity there.

Also the more I think about any one instance of my thought, the less I understand how memory, knowledge, belief, idea, and emotion might be connected at that moment. For example, if walking outside, I might have a memory of this view, knowledge about the flora and fauna there, and any number of positive or negative emotions about the experience, and yet none of them might be at the forefront of my mind, which tends to follow whatever sequence of words I'm thinking to myself at that point. I might be aware that my mood or memory has an atmosphere or ancillary parts, but I have little conception of how they all link together.

With meditation, even if I cannot discover the parts of my mind, at least I'm able to refocus the series of thoughts to follow for example an algorithm or a scientific idea that requires not a jumble of mental phenomena, but an accurate movement from one point to another. A few minutes of such ratiocination taxes me, and embarrasses me for how little of my waking life I devote to it. It is not that emotion and belief are bad and that I should aspire to be like only Spock, but that emotion and belief tend to be tumultuous, haphazard, and inaccurate, often things I'd like to avoid.

Another related topic is the disappointing degree to which suburban America has inured itself to lawn care noises. If a person lives in a neighborhood with some twenty houses in audible distance, each with a front and backyard full of grass that requires a mower and weeder, and if one's neighbors are diligent about their maintenance, then that can be any number of hours of motor as daily ambience. Electric woodworking machinery and neglected dogs that like to bark at mowers and machinery are other noisy hobbies. If a television or headphones are on, or one is on the phone, or even if one doesn't even pay attention to the noise, perhaps it is not a noticeable issue, and one easily dismissed as minor or worth the pleasure of the maintenance. I never noticed it before I started meditation. Now every time a mower starts, I'm apoplectic.

In researching municipal residential noise ordinances, I learned that lawn maintenance during daylight hours is acceptable. A few people have written op-eds about their displeasure. Overall though, so many hours of noise fail to impinge on the awareness of many, or at least in any meaningful way that might change legislation. Why the enterprise doesn't go electric, is beyond me.

I believe there are neighborhoods with associations full of people who are stringent about their sonic atmospheres. I wonder if the more I study my dispositions, the less I will feel in control and more I will become conservative. On a positive note, the hours at night are silent, but there is no sunlight.
