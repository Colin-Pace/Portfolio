16 May 2020

The focus of contemporary anthropology is to laud multiculturalism and condemn ethnocentrism. An anthropologist does so by writing articles for journals or monographic texts called ethnographies. The content of the writing is an expostulation of culture and society. The former is the beliefs that a people have about a subject, and the latter is the structure of their relationships to one another such as citizen to citizen or employer to employee. The result is an ethnography of a group of people which shows a society and culture, and praises its multiculturalism and castigates its ethnocentrism. Much of the work is figuring out the relevant lists and sub lists of beliefs and relations that are relevant for a group and what the various sub groups and their connections and overlays are; and then making a presentation in clear prose.

Anthropologists construct their texts about power with rhetoric. Just as the founder of liberal arts Aristotle noted that pathos, ethos, and logos are the three types of rhetorical argument available for persuasion, so the founder of Germanic sociology Max Weber noted that there are three types of power in society: charisma, tradition, and rationality. The Aristotelian and Weberian paradigms roughly correspond, more with the second and third types. Because the first two types of each paradigm are salient in many societies, so an anthropologist is often put in the awkward position of literarily including a culture or society that is excluded or discriminated against, but one that also makes discriminatory claims against others, whether through its tradition or demagoguery.

Since finding the correct balance of information and tone in an ethnography is troubled by the exposition of illogical claims, globalization offers itself as an easy and attractive amelioration. In theory, any group of people can replace their connections of identity from a local to a global context. The support of universal human rights, is an example. With such a globalized group as the subject of an ethnography, the anthropologist also avoids frustrating many non elites of the First World who find themselves otherwise having to laud or watch the celebration of on the global stage the local culture of other places, often for political correctness and to the detriment of their own values. The frustration sustained, is what led to the resurgence of conservative nationalism in recent years.

Some anthropology avoids these issues altogether. Instead it focuses on the presentation of information as an introduction to people who are relatively unknown in international media. This group of a few hundred or thousand, has this unique way of worship, cuisine, or entertainment, and because it is unknown, therefore its members are interesting and illustrative of the diversity of humanity. This genre of anthropology often avoids idealizing as exotic the subject, a trope that characterizes travel writing, even though an audience often seeks such a literary adventure. Instead the reader finds that life is as mundane and boring elsewhere as it is here. Another development is the training and employment in the US of people from other nations, who then report on a culture or society from their own or another country. The insider perspective has its own advantages and disadvantages, both for the understanding of the local context and the writing of ethnography.

Traditionally, the three categories of analysis in anthropology have been race, class, and gender. Each presents an enormous amount of conceptual and empirical complexity. The National Human Genome Research Institute among other sources, has shown that the allele frequency which gives phenotypical variation, is organized by clinal distribution. The sections of the genome that encode a phenotype, are relatively recently evolved and few in number, making them fluctuate to a greater extent and more rapidly than those long evolved genes that often in the thousands together create a trait like intelligence. The strongest argument in the anthropological toolkit for the commonality of humanity, is therefore at the same time an example of an awkward instance when the anthropologist has to inform an audience that their conception of, for example, being a white person, is ill founded insofar as they believe that a phenotypical variation corresponds with a biological determination of behavior, cognitive or otherwise. The misinformed context arises because people with that allele distribution, and others that might correspond, develop a culture and society, and then presume that the biological difference accounts for the cultural or social difference; the belief and subsequent enactment of behavior as if that were true, is what results in racism. The belief is as easily dispelled as showing the history or geographic variation in culture or society of the same race of people. The culture and society of white people in France is not that of those in the US, just as neither of their cultures or societies are the same today as they were two hundred or two thousand years ago. It can be achieved also by showing that a person of one race can act like that of another. Without being a geneticist, the anthropologist can risk a genetic argument for the explanation of the equality of humanity, that clinal distribution does not correspond with race. Or a fourth recourse is highlighting the illogical collection of races with nationalities, as if they were all of the same category and biologically determined. Despite the abundance of evidence against the concept of race as an accurate biological category, nonetheless the amount of vitriol and obscurity of the ideas, combined with the ingrained and unspoken nature of their practice in civilization, makes the deconstructions of the philosophy of race, still difficult to explain and accept.

The complexities for class are also naturalized. The naturalization of what is historically contingent, is an anthropological phrase, jargon, that explains a historical trend whereby a belief about a social or cultural behavior gradually is thought to be caused by the genetics of a person or group of people. As Pierre Bourdieu shows in Distinction, a pattern and valuation of bodily hexis, speech, furniture arrangement, meal preparation, art viewership, and perception of nearby space are evidenced by how one is taught to live with them; and the patterns and valuations correspond, at least among Parisians but probably elsewhere as well, with classes that can be measured by one's title and that of one's father. As the traditions gradually develop among classes over time, and those of the wealthy become more articulated among a larger group of people and portrayed throughout a larger geographic area by media, people begin to believe that the patterns and valuations correspond to bloodlines, aristocratic families, rather than occur as the result of the collection of instances of teaching and mimicry that become second nature in a person's behavior due to a long duration of exposure. Aside from dispelling such misconceptions, whether by showing historical and geographic vicissitudes or cross class identities, anthropologists also dabble in economic theory, at least to suggest that those practices of capitalism that are chaotic and result in vast differences in income and wealth, have pernicious effects on the lifestyles and beliefs of different classes. Sometimes there is an explicit call for this or that system. Whether the Krugmans of the world, find anthropological economics valid and sound, is another question. Perhaps a redeeming quality, is the connection between ethnographic vignette and economic statistics. The stories bring to life, vivify, economic abstractions. The balance of the Neoclassical Synthesis between the regulation of the ethics of the free market and its sensitivities to consumer wants and needs, is consistent with the progressive anthropological vanguard.

Gender too is beset by complexity. The definition of the concept shows as much. It can refer either to biological differences, typically the two of dimorphism, but it is quickly shown that this idea too is inaccurate, and also social and cultural differences, what the terms masculinity and femininity denote. Sexuality is another axis of analysis that adds complexity. When combined what results is a figuration of a person with a sex, perhaps any number of masculine or feminine qualities, and a preference for a partner with a unique figuration; and any component or its connection to the others, can change over time, despite the naturalization of dispositions. Anthropological ethnography catalogs the various beliefs and traditions of gender, and also demonstrates that beliefs about correspondence between biology and then culture and society are inaccurate insofar as the former does not determine the latter, even if people use the former as a plane of difference for which to elaborate the latter. The three categories, race, class, and gender, are still the mainstay of anthropology, but they have been remodulated and through metonymy put in the background.

A few other literary tropes characterize contemporary anthropology. In no particular order, they are plurality, historicization, and aesthetics. The trend toward plurality is intended to show that a given category of a culture or society contains a typology of that category. Its method is the enumeration of lists, an effort of documentation that shows an example of the multitude and flexibility of humanity, and also the philosophical conclusion that because of the plurality, therefore ethnocentrism that promotes singularity should be jettisoned because it is thereby discriminatory. The goal is laudable. But the tone of the expostulation borders on chastisement, and because the anthropologist is rarely an expert in the minutia of a culture or society, therefore sometimes also inaccurate, which adds an unpleasant valence to the hectoring. It is also curious since most of the audience is already on the same political side as the anthropologist. The historicization of a topic displays the often ethnocentric and colonial legacy that led to a contemporary belief, with the goal that the association of the legacy with ethnocentrism should attenuate the ideology. Like the economist who might wonder about anthropological economics, so the historian might wonder about anthropological history, which eschews technicality in favor of often the grand view and eclectic sources. But when done well, like with anthropological economics, the result can be persuasive, and a reader learns both information and has their conceptual contours remolded such that the view of multiculturalism is expanded and enforced emotionally. The depiction of aesthetics, the appreciation of beauty, is emerging from the deconstructionist method of contemporary anthropology, one imagines because anthropologists must become depressed with the interminable review and condemnation of ethnocentrism. The trope is a sort of revival, once called salvage anthropology, and like that of the catalogers involves the attempt to portray variations across cultures and societies less as exotic pieces of fascination, and more as pinnacles of human achievement that can be appreciated by people from other cultures and societies as other, interesting ways of doing things.

Only a few meta lists have been compiled, showing the relative citations of anthropological articles and ethnographies by anthropologists in their articles and ethnographies. The meta lists have been ignored by the flagship journal. Although continually focused on themselves, reflective, anthropologists take a genre, identity, or place as the grounds for self scrutiny, rather than the tendencies of the discipline as a whole. Because of the lacuna, therefore there is little standardization of the curriculum taught by the faculty across anthropological traditions or even within one. Loosely structured with respect to object and method, anthropology is less of a science than many social scientists would like to let on. Yet so too, it is neither an art form nor a speculative discipline in the humanities. Anthropology is also one of the largest undergraduate majors in the nation. Because of the amorphous nature of study and the encompassing reach of its curriculum, which has to contend with the uncomfortable inclusion of often discriminatory ideologies even if not explicitly so recognized, in addition to teaching the goals of lauding multiculturalism and condemning ethnocentrism, there might be a salubrious creation of political values to be paired with the expostulation of the aesthetic achievements of people across the world. Then all the anthropologically trained, whatever their profession turns out to be, would have a guiding light for political commitments, and a theoretical clarity for how to deal with a plethora of topics that contain both positivity and negativity.
